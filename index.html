Computer science is the study of computation, automation, and information. Computer science spans a range of topics from theoretical studies of algorithms and the limits of computation to practical issues of implementing computing systems in hardware and software.[1]

Computer science addresses any computing problems or information processes, especially within the context of a computer system. Subfields include programming language theory, which deals with the design and implementation of languages for computing; human-computer interaction focuses on making computers and computations useful, usable, and universally accessible to people; and artificial intelligence aims to construct synthetic computers and programs that can display or simulate intelligent behavior.

1. History

The history of computer science began long before the modern discipline of computer science that emerged in the twentieth century, and was rooted in the study of algorithm and mathematics. The earliest foundations of what would become computer science predate the invention of the modern digital computer. Machines for calculating fixed numerical tasks, such as the abacus, have existed since antiquity, but they served only a practical purpose and did not represent a theoretical approach to computing.

The formalization of computer science as a discipline began in the 1950s. The first university degree in computer science was offered by the University of Purdue in 1962. Since then, computer science has become a prominent academic field, with numerous universities offering undergraduate and graduate degrees.

2. Etymology

Despite its name, computer science is not strictly a science in the empirical sense of the word, which is concerned with the study of natural or physical phenomena. Rather, computer science is an engineering discipline, which deals with systematic collection, analysis, synthesis, and application of knowledge for the design and development of computing systems and processes.

3. Philosophy

There are several different perspectives on the nature of computer science. Many computer scientists see themselves as belonging to one or more of several broad categories: theorists who develop algorithms and analyze their correctness and complexity; system designers who build hardware and software; practitioners who apply known techniques in new contexts; and researchers who expand the boundaries of computer science through both theory and practice.

4. Areas of computer science 4.1 Theory of computation

According to Peter J. Denning, the fundamental question underlying computer science is, "What can be automated?" The study of the theory of computation is focused on answering this question. It involves the study of algorithms, data structures, computational complexity theory, and information theory.

4.2 Artificial intelligence

Artificial intelligence (AI) aims to or has aimed to implement human or animal-like intelligence in machines. It encompasses the study of logic, knowledge representation, planning, learning, language processing, perception, and robotics.

4.3 Computer graphics and visualization

Computer graphics is the study of digital visual contents, and involves synthesizing and manipulating images. It is closely related to computer-aided design (CAD) and computational geometry, both of which are concerned with modeling and representing physical objects and environments.

5. Research and industry

Computer science research is conducted in universities and research laboratories, as well as in corporate research facilities. Research funding in the United States comes from the National Science Foundation, the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency, and other agencies. The field also benefits from significant private sector investment.

6. Ethics

Computer science raises ethical questions both in the practice of the profession and in the impacts of technology on society. Issues include the right to privacy, the responsible use of technology, and the consequences of automation on employment. Many professional organizations have adopted codes of ethics that members are expected to uphold.
